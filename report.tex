\begin{abstract}
We compare two intersection control schemes -- a four-arm roundabout and a pair of opposing-phase traffic lights -- on identical road geometry, using a custom Python simulation in which each car follows a Lennard-Jones-style braking law and is spawned as a Poisson process at the entrance of an arm. We sweep traffic volume, asymmetric loading, and signal cycle length and measure the mean passing time and the total exit flow rate. In the configuration tested, the traffic light is faster than the roundabout at every density studied, with the gap growing from $\sim 0$~s at very light traffic to roughly $30$~s at heavy traffic. The roundabout, however, exhibits a striking direction-dependent bias: under one-way traffic on perpendicular arms, vertical-arm cars wait $20$--$40$~s longer than horizontal-arm cars because of the yield-to-the-left rule. This effect is qualitatively the same as the rush-hour bias commonly observed at the Armdale Roundabout in Halifax. The traffic light is essentially fair (anti-symmetric in $H \leftrightarrow V$) and saturates the total exit flow at $\sim 0.36$~cars/s against $\sim 0.15$~cars/s for the roundabout.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
What would happen if we replaced all traffic lights with roundabouts? Would you wait longer? This is what our project aims to investigate by simulating a single four-arm intersection in Python and comparing two control schemes side by side on identical geometry.

The simulation tracks individual cars with continuous position, velocity, and acceleration. The two performance metrics we care about are
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{mean passing time} -- the time elapsed between a car spawning at the road boundary and exiting on the far side of the intersection;
    \item \textbf{exit flow rate} -- the number of cars that successfully clear the intersection per unit of simulation time, summed over the two emitting arms.
\end{itemize}

We control the system through a small set of parameters that fall into two natural groups. The model parameters describing how each driver behaves are held fixed across all runs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item desired cruising speed $v_{\text{target}} = 11$~m/s
    \item velocity relaxation time $\tau = 2$~s
    \item Lennard-Jones interaction range $\sigma = 7$~m on the arms, ring traversal speed $5.5$~m/s
    \item integration time step $\Delta t = 0.05$--$0.1$~s
\end{itemize}

The intersection-level parameters are the ones we vary to ask physical questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item car spawn rate per arm
    \item asymmetry between the horizontal and vertical spawn rates
    \item traffic-light cycle period
    \item roundabout radius and yield threshold
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this report is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:method} describes the model, the simulation algorithm, and the assumptions we made. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents simulation results across symmetric, asymmetric, and varying-cycle conditions. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} interprets the data and discusses practical implications. The full source code is in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-code}.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Assumptions}
We work with a deliberately stripped-down model so that the comparison between roundabout and traffic light is clean.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Each road has a single lane in the direction of travel. Opposite-direction traffic on the same arm is not modelled (the project handout explicitly allows this).
    \item Cars maintain their direction through the intersection -- no turns. Horizontal traffic enters from the west arm and exits east; vertical traffic enters from the south arm and exits north.
    \item Cars do not overtake on a given road.
    \item Driver behaviour is identical for all cars (uniform $v_{\text{target}}$, $\tau$, $\sigma$).
    \item No yellow phase: the traffic light is purely red/green, switching every $T_{\text{cycle}}/2$.
    \item Spawning is a Poisson process with constant rate, with a $10$~m minimum-gap rejection at the entrance to suppress unphysical pile-ups.
    \item No pedestrians, weather, lane changes, or driver errors.
    \item Both control schemes use the \emph{same} road geometry: each arm is $95$~m long approaching the intersection and $35$~m long leaving it, and the cars cross either a single point (lights) or a ring of radius $15$~m (roundabout).
\end{enumerate}

These assumptions reduce realism but isolate the geometric and rule-based differences between the two control schemes.

\subsection{Car-following model}
Each car has a position $s$ along its road, a longitudinal velocity $v$, and a target velocity $v_{\text{target}}$. The longitudinal acceleration is
\begin{equation}
    a = \frac{v_{\text{target}} - v}{\tau} \;-\; \left(\frac{\sigma}{R}\right)^{12} v ,
    \label{eq:lj}
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the gap to the obstacle ahead -- either a leading car or, when applicable, a stop line. The first term is a relaxation toward target speed; the second is the Lennard-Jones brake from the project handout, which grows extremely quickly when $R$ falls below $\sigma$. We integrate forward in Euler steps of $\Delta t = 0.05$~s for the lights system and $\Delta t = 0.1$~s for the roundabout. Velocities are clipped at zero so cars never reverse.

In the roundabout (Figure~\ref{fig:setup_round}) the central ring has radius $R_{\text{ring}}=15$~m. Each car is described in polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$ and moves through a small state machine: \texttt{approach} on its entry arm (decreasing $r$), \texttt{circle} inside the ring (constant $r$, increasing $\theta$), \texttt{exit} on its outgoing arm (increasing $r$), and \texttt{done} once it leaves the simulation boundary. A vehicle in \texttt{approach} state may transition to \texttt{circle} only if no vehicle in the ring is within an angular distance $\sim \pi/3$ counter-clockwise of the entry point -- the standard yield-to-the-left rule. Inside the ring vehicles cruise at a target tangential speed of $5.5$~m/s and follow each other using the same Lennard-Jones interaction in arc-length space. They exit the ring once they have travelled the angular distance to their assigned exit and continue onto the corresponding outgoing arm.

In the traffic-light setup (Figure~\ref{fig:setup_lights}) two perpendicular roads cross at the origin. Horizontal and vertical signals are exactly $180^{\circ}$ out of phase (when one is red, the other is green). A red light is treated by an approaching car as a stationary obstacle at the stop line; once the light turns green the obstacle disappears and the car accelerates back to $v_{\text{target}}$.

\subsection{Measurements}
We track two numbers per simulation run:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean passing time} $\langle t \rangle$: the average over all cars that exited during the run, of $t_{\text{exit}} - t_{\text{spawn}}$. We discard cars that finished before a $50$~s warm-up window so that initial transients do not bias the mean.
    \item \textbf{Exit flow rate} $\Phi$: the total number of post-warm-up exits divided by the post-warm-up simulation time, summed over both directions.
\end{itemize}
We sweep across spawn rates from $0.05$ to $0.80$~cars/s/road and across cycle periods from $5$ to $80$~s. Each sweep point is a single simulation of $T = 200$--$300$~s with a fixed RNG seed.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Symmetric traffic}
Figure~\ref{fig:sym_density} shows the mean passing time as a function of spawn rate when both roads carry the same load. Both control schemes start near $\sim 18$--$20$~s at low density (the time for a free-flowing car to travel the $\sim 130$~m arm-plus-intersection-plus-exit at $\sim 11$~m/s, plus a small overhead). The traffic light grows gently, reaching only $\sim 27$~s at $0.80$~cars/s. The roundabout grows much more sharply -- doubling by $0.40$~cars/s and saturating near $\sim 60$~s for higher loads. The saturation is because the V-arm queue grows beyond the simulated road, so fewer cars actually reach the finish line in the simulation window.

    \centering
    \begin{subfigure}[t]{0.49\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_analysis2_roundabout_density.png}
        \caption{Roundabout: passing time and throughput vs.\ spawn rate.}
        \label{fig:sym_round}
    \end{subfigure}
    \hfill
    \begin{subfigure}[t]{0.49\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_analysis5_lights_density_and_cycle.png}
        \caption{Traffic light: passing time vs.\ density (left) and vs.\ cycle period at fixed density (right).}
        \label{fig:sym_lights}
    \end{subfigure}
    \caption{Symmetric loading: mean passing time as a function of spawn rate, plus auxiliary plots showing throughput (roundabout) and cycle dependence (lights).}
    \label{fig:sym_density}
\end{figure}

The cycle-period sweep on the right of Figure~\ref{fig:sym_lights} answers the project's traffic-light follow-up question. At a fixed density of $0.4$~cars/s, the mean passing time is U-shaped in cycle period: it minimises near $T_{\text{cycle}} \approx 15$--$20$~s. Very short cycles (5~s) waste time switching back and forth, so cars never build momentum across the intersection. Very long cycles (60--80~s) trap the queueing direction in long red phases. The optimum reflects the trade-off between switching overhead and queue-buildup overhead.

The headline numerical comparison is in Figure~\ref{fig:headtohead}. Across every density studied, the traffic light beats the roundabout: the gap is small at low density ($\sim 0$~s at $0.05$~cars/s) and grows substantially at moderate density (up to $\sim 33$~s at $0.5$--$0.6$~cars/s). The throughput plot in Figure~\ref{fig:flow} shows the same picture: the lights saturate at $\sim 0.36$~cars/s of total exit flow while the roundabout caps out near $\sim 0.15$~cars/s.

The gap is negative everywhere -- the light is faster at every density tested -- and grows dramatically once the roundabout starts to bottleneck.}
    \label{fig:headtohead}
\end{figure}

For the roundabout, fixing $r_V$ and increasing $r_H$ barely changes the mean passing time -- the H-stream always has priority because of the CCW yield rule. Conversely, fixing $r_H$ and increasing $r_V$ substantially raises the mean. This breaks the $H \leftrightarrow V$ symmetry one would naively expect from a four-fold symmetric geometry. For the lights, the contour is nearly diagonal-symmetric: the signal alternates between the two directions, so on average both streams see the same fraction of green time.

The asymmetry is even more visible when we plot the difference $\langle t \rangle_H - \langle t \rangle_V$ directly (Figure~\ref{fig:hv_diff}).

For the roundabout, the $H - V$ map is negative everywhere and reaches $\sim -38$~s in the high-traffic corner. For the light, the difference is roughly anti-symmetric: $H$ cars are slower when there is heavy $H$ traffic and faster when the V-direction is loaded, with a smooth zero-crossing along the diagonal. The light treats both directions identically; the roundabout, by construction, does not.

\subsection{Flow-rate comparison}
Figure~\ref{fig:flow} shows total exit flow as a function of spawn rate, plus the throughput efficiency (exit flow divided by twice the per-arm spawn rate).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig_analysis9_flow_rate_comparison.png}
    \caption{Throughput comparison. Left: total exit flow vs.\ spawn rate. The roundabout saturates near $0.15$~cars/s as soon as the V-arm starts to back up; the light continues to grow with spawn rate up to $\sim 0.36$~cars/s. Right: efficiency (exit flow divided by $2 \times$ spawn rate). Both schemes track $100$~\% efficiency at low load; the roundabout breaks down rapidly above $r \approx 0.10$, while the lights remain $> 30$~\% efficient even at $0.6$~cars/s.}
    \label{fig:flow}
\end{figure}

At low density both schemes pass nearly every car that arrives. Above $\sim 0.15$~cars/s the roundabout begins to bottleneck on the V-arm; by $0.30$~cars/s its exit flow has saturated and additional spawned cars simply pile up. The light maintains a far higher saturation flow because its alternating-phase rule does not introduce a permanent bias.

\section{Conclusion and Discussion}\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Summary of findings}
The simulation cleanly answers each of the project's six suggested analysis questions.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Roundabout passing time at moderate density.} At $r_H = r_V = 0.3$~cars/s, the roundabout mean passing time is $\sim 52$~s overall ($H = 40$~s, $V = 75$~s), demonstrating an immediate $\sim 35$~s asymmetry.
    \item \textbf{Density dependence.} Mean passing time grows monotonically with density on the roundabout, sharply steepening above $r \approx 0.2$ as the V-arm queue overflows the simulated road.
    \item \textbf{Asymmetric heatmap.} The roundabout contour is not symmetric. Vertical-side density has a much stronger effect than horizontal-side density, because the CCW yield rule favours horizontal traffic.
    \item \textbf{$H - V$ bias.} The bias reaches $\sim -38$~s in favour of $H$ at the heaviest tested load -- analogous to the rush-hour Armdale Roundabout bias the project introduction mentions.
    \item \textbf{Repeating with a traffic light.} The light is faster than the roundabout at every density tested (Figure~\ref{fig:headtohead}). Its $H - V$ heatmap is anti-symmetric across the diagonal; the asymmetric passing time is roughly symmetric in $(r_H, r_V)$. The cycle-period sweep finds an optimum around $15$--$20$~s.
    \item \textbf{When does the roundabout win?} In this configuration -- one-way traffic on each arm, no opposing-direction traffic, fixed yield-to-the-left rule -- the answer in our simulation is \emph{never}. The light beats the roundabout at every spawn rate from $0.05$ to $0.80$~cars/s. This is a configuration-specific finding; in a real Halifax intersection, opposing-direction traffic and turning movements would change the picture, and roundabouts have other advantages (lower severe-collision rate, no signal maintenance, no power requirements) that lie outside the scope of the mean-passing-time / throughput metric we measured.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What this means in practice}
Three caveats are important when interpreting this conclusion. First, our roundabout uses one-way traffic on each arm only, so there is no opposing direction and the yield-to-the-left rule has no chance of becoming symmetric. Real roundabouts circulate traffic from all four arms simultaneously, which restores some of the symmetry the model is missing -- and in fact our \texttt{run\_roundabout\_sim} function supports this directly via its \texttt{opposite=True} flag. Second, our light has an idealised cycle (no yellow phase, perfectly regular). In practice yellow phases and pedestrian-detector logic eat into the green time. Third, our cars all travel at the same target speed and react identically, which is unrealistically uniform.

Within those limits, the answer is that traffic lights are more efficient on average for one-way perpendicular traffic, but the roundabout would catch up in scenarios where the light's signal overhead becomes punishing -- for example, very low traffic where there is no need to alternate, or very heavy traffic where the cycle penalty is amortised away. Our data does not show such a crossover in the range tested.

The most physically interesting result is the $H - V$ bias on the roundabout (Figure~\ref{fig:hv_round}). It reproduces qualitatively the bias the project introduction mentions for the Armdale Roundabout: one direction is favoured because of the geometry of the yield rule, and that bias grows with traffic intensity. This is exactly the kind of effect one cannot predict from intuition alone -- it required a simulation to see.
